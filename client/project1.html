Hunters Point Shipyard Artists

The Hunters Point Shipyard Artists (HPSA) is an organization of about 250 artists who rent studios in a former US naval base in San Francisco. The HPSA was looking to engage with the larger community while providing exposure to their art. At the same time, community members who support the artists' work wanted an opportunity to engage more intimately with the artists.

Tools & Methods

information architecture, user interviews, surveys, contextual inquiries, rapid prototyping, interaction design, concept design, design studios, sketching, affinity mapping, empathy mapping, personas, service design

The Problem

The Hunters Point Shipyard Artists (HPSA) website caters to artists as well as the people who support their art, but it fails to connect and engage these two groups

The website (www.shipyardartists.com) provides information about Open Studio, a large event that takes place twice a year. The website also gives users year-round access to the artist directory. However, the HPSA would like to provide more exposure to their art while engaging with people on an ongoing basis, not just at the very popular (and crowded) Open Studio events. Furthermore, artists want to develop better connections with their fellow HPSA community members.

With this in mind, my team and I sought out to validate and understand the problem from multiple perspectives.

The Solution

Take a service design approach to allow users to attend small, intimate events with the shipyard artists while ensuring that the artists maintain control over the event.

Before developing a solution to the problem, we had to make sure that the needs of all users of the HPSA website were met in a user-centered manner. Since the goal was to foster more engagement and connection between the HPSA and their supporters in the community, the solution had to be bilateral.

Our idea to address HPSAâ€™s problem was to emulate the crowdfunding idea that sites like Kickstarter use to fund projects. Instead of funding financially, visitors to the HPSA website would fund small and intimate events by simply joining them. These small events would require a minimum number of participants in order for it to take place. In the event page, users can see how many participants have already joined the event and how many are still needed for the event to be official. This concept also prompts the user to share the event with friends so they can make sure it goes through.

This idea addresses both the artists' needs as well as the community supporters' by creating an engagement loop in the website. Of course, donations will also be encouraged.

The Process

Agile approach with user and business at the center.

Research Strategy

We performed contextual inquiries with the artists in the shipyard in order to understand the motivations and pain points of the website's core users. After gathering a robust amount of data from these inquiries, we proceeded to explore these same concepts but from the eyes of the website's users who were looking to support or learn about the HPSA. For this, we used interviews and surveys.



Synthesizing the Data

So, we had to make sense of the large amount of information we had gathered. To tackle this, my teammates and I created affinity maps that showed us key patterns within our data, empathy maps which helped us understand the feelings and thoughts our users had while they used the HPSA website, and journey maps to help tie it all together. We condensed these findings to create primary personas that would guide our design decisions. We created Barcelona to represent the HPSA, and Cassandra to represent the supporting community.
